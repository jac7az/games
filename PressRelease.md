# Headline: Beyond A Score - What Actually Drives Video Game Success?
## Hook
In an industry where all the AAA titles can cost more than even Hollywood movies to produce, developers and investors are hooked on chasing that high Metacritic score. The assumption is widely used, where high critical scores equal financial success. However, new analysis challenges this assumption, where researchers investigated thousands of game records to see just how influential high scores are in getting that sweet financial success.

## Problem Statement
Currently, the video game market is saturated with thousands of releases in a year, making it harder for quality titles to stand out more than ever. Many studios operate under the assumption that higher quality=higher revenue. However, analysis reveals a significant disconnect between what critics rate highly and what actually gets sold. The specific problem then becomes clear. Is a high production quality, as reflected by the critic score, still the most reliable predictor in sales success, or are other factors now more influential?

## Solution Description
To answer this question, researchers constructed a random forest regressor model, inputting factors like publisher, developer, console availability, and release year to build the model and see which features have the most influence over total sales and which don't. The solution illustrates a better picture of what truly influences the games market, allowing stakeholders to better understand what to prioritize and give a peek for new gamers at how to search for new games to play.

## Chart
The chart below illustrates the weight, or the amount of influence, the predictors have on video game sales. The developer power is the overwhelming leader in predicting sales, holding more than double the influence compared to critic scores. The developer is specifically the company that produced the game, which is separate from the company or site that published it for public release, which would be the publisher. In fact, the publisher itself actually holds very little influence on determining total sales of a game. However, that doesn't mean the critic score is completely useless. It still lies within the top 3, overtaking other factors like release year or console.

Overall, reputation and platform are often more useful in gaining traction compared to the actual critic score. However, while a high score isn't THE deciding, guarantee win factor, it still remains a critical metric for success that users interested in new games should look out for. This is especially true for new game titles that are from smaller or indie developers rather than the big names dominating the market. There's always something new to try out, and one might never know where they will find some hidden gems.
